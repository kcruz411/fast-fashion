Clothing In Our Landfills

The clothing and textiles industry is the second-most polluter in the world, so it's no wonder we find it all throughout our ever-growing landfills. The clothing that ends up in landfills can sit there for 200-plus years, and as it decomposes, it emits methane—a greenhouse gas more potent than carbon. Much of what we donate gets packed, shipped, and re-sold overseas or gets dumped in landfills anyway. Right now, less than 1% of collected clothing is recycled into new yarns and fibers. New technologies, like chemical recycling (which separates raw materials from dyes, elastics, etc. for effective use as new textiles) are being developed, but we have a long way to go until they’re fully developed and put into mainstream practice.



Approximately 15% of fabric intended for clothing ends up in waste sites, never being used for clothing at all.

Discarded clothing made of non-biodegradable


fabrics can sit in landfills for up to 200 years.

In the rare textile recycling programs that exist, about 85% of this waste goes to landfills.
